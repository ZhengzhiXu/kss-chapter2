Definition of Learning

Learning is any relatively durable change in behavior or knowledge that is due to experience.

Why is learning important?

  • Personal habits (e.g., nailbiting)
  • Personality traits (e.g., shyness)
  • Personal preferences (e.g., distaste for formal clothes)
  • Emotional responses (e.g., preference for rap music)

Classical Conditioning

Definition

Classical Conditioning is a type of learning in which a stimulus acquires the capacity to evoke a response that was originally evoked by another stimulus.

Ivan Pavlov

  • ​Russian Physiologist
  • Found that dogs learn to salivate to simply the sounds that they regularly hear before being fed

  • Developed classical conditioning: ​ neutral stimuli associated with stimuli such as food will produce similar responses as the old stimuli

Unconditioned Stimulus (UCS):

a stimulus that evokes an unconditioned response without previous conditioning

Unconditioned Response (UCR):

an unlearned reaction to an unconditioned stimulus that occurs without previous conditioning

Conditioned Stimulus (CS):

a previously neutral stimulus that has – through pairing with an unconditioned stimulus – acquired the capacity to evoke a conditioned response

Conditioned Response (CR):

a learned reaction to a conditioned stimulus that occurs because of previous conditioning (may be the same as the unconditioned response)

What is the UCS, UCR, CS, CR in Pavlov's dog case?

Acquisition

  • ​learning has occurred once the animals respond to the CS without the US
  • repeated pairings of CS and US yield a stronger CR
  • most effective conditioning: ​ - present US first
    • introduce US while CS is still evident

Little Albert Experiment

Video:
The aim of Watson and Rayner was to condition a phobia in an emotionally stable child.

Result: ​Conditioned Albert (a little boy) to fear a white rat

  • When Albert was playing with a white rat → experimenter makes very loud noise every time Albert touches the rat → Albert start to fear the white rat

Generalization

  • Albert generalized to other fluffy white things
